About Me

Why Financial
Literacy Matters to Me

Growing up, financial decision making was regularly discussed in my family. My parents explained investing, loans, credit cards, and budgeting the same way other families might talk about the week's news. I assumed every family had these conversations.

When I entered college at Auburn, I quickly realized that many students my age had never had the same exposure. Tracking spending, understanding an IRA, avoiding compounding debt — these felt routine to me, but foreign to many classmates. One friend casually mentioned he already had nearly a thousand dollars in credit card debt only months after high school graduation, as if it were simply normal.

These were not careless people. They were people who had never been taught what to look for. The difference between us was not intelligence or responsibility — it was access.

That access should not be a matter of luck or family background. This portfolio collects my research and writing exploring how financial literacy gaps form, what they cost young Americans, and what can be done to fix them.

Research Proposal

The Financial Literacy Issue

January 21, 2026

Every year, all forms of consumer debt increase in the United States, which often ties individuals down to their poor financial decisions. These poor decisions frequently occur when individuals are entering college or shortly after, as they transition into the real world.

In U.S. public high schools today, only around 26–31% of students take any form of financial literacy coursework. This early failure allows students entering college to make harmful decisions: taking on excess student loan debt, mismanaging credit cards, neglecting investments, and budgeting poorly.

The longer you invest, the less likely you are to lose money

Source: Madi Manages Money · Yahoo Finance & S&P Dow Jones Indices

"The average person in Generation Z already carries around $4,000 in compounding credit card debt. Financially illiterate people often do not budget correctly — they are tied down to pressing short-term expenses while the debt grows silently in the background."

Key Research Questions

  • What is the current scale of financial illiteracy among college students in America?
  • At what stage in life do harmful financial habits most often form?
  • What institutional and individual solutions exist or could be created?
  • How do other countries address financial literacy, and what can the U.S. learn?

Research Methods

  • Auburn University library database — peer-reviewed studies and statistics
  • Web research from federal agencies, nonprofits, and financial organizations
  • Professional interviews with wealth management advisors at Morgan Stanley

Problem/Solution Paper

Creating Sustainable Financial Habits for Prolonged Wealth in College Students

April 2, 2026

Every year, all forms of debt increase in the United States, and a significant portion is carried by young adults who never had the chance to learn how to avoid it. The moment most Americans gain financial independence — roughly when they enter college — they face credit cards, student loans, lease agreements, and bills that no one ever taught them to manage.

In American public high schools today, only around twenty-six percent of students have access to any form of financial literacy coursework. That number barely improves in college. Unless a student pursues a degree in finance or accounting, the odds of receiving formal education in personal financial management are slim.

"American colleges and universities should implement a mandatory, two-credit online financial literacy course for all first-year students — directly addressing the institutional gaps that have allowed this crisis to persist."

The 50/30/20 Rule: allocating income across needs, wants, and savings

Source: WealthSync Services

The Scale of the Problem

The average member of Generation Z carries around $3,500 in compounding credit card debt. For someone who does not understand compound interest, that balance grows silently while they focus on more immediate pressures. Research by Davila and Hilliard found that students with lower financial literacy accumulate more debt and are far less aware of the long-term cost of their borrowing decisions.

Compound interest starts as pocket change — and becomes the primary value of savings

Source: A Learning a Day

The Proposed Solution

A mandatory accredited two-credit online pass/fail course — covering debt, budgeting, loans, investing, and savings — would ensure every incoming student receives a financial foundation before they face decisions that define their early adult years. The online format makes it accessible regardless of schedule or major. At two credits, it is a modest investment with an outsized return.

Anthony Barst, a financial advisor at Morgan Stanley, noted in an interview that many of his clients' college-aged children regularly approach him with basic questions about debt and budgeting — questions he believes should have been answered in a classroom years earlier.

Autobiographical Essay

Why Financial Literacy Is Important to Me

February 2, 2026

Growing up, financial decision making was often talked about in my family. My parents regularly discussed investing, loans, credit cards, and budgeting — at the dinner table, on the couch, in passing. They explained why strong financial literacy matters and how poor financial literacy would affect me later in life.

When I entered college, I quickly realized that many students my age had never had this same exposure. One friend told me he already had close to a thousand dollars in credit card debt only months after graduating high school, and said it so casually, as though it were inevitable.

"These were not careless people. They were people who had never been taught what to look for. The difference between us was not intelligence or responsibility — it was access."

Looking into the numbers, I learned that only around a third of U.S. public high school students take a financial literacy class. Most students graduate without learning how to budget, invest, or spend properly. Even in college, unless students major in finance or accounting, they will likely never take a personal financial management course.

For many students, college is the first time they pay rent, buy groceries, or manage bills alone. Without guidance, small mistakes grow quickly. What starts as a manageable credit card balance can snowball into thousands of dollars of high-interest debt — creating stress that affects mental health, physical health, career prospects, and relationships.

My background gives me a unique perspective. Growing up with financial education at home, I understand what effective financial literacy looks like in real-world decisions. At the same time, being in college and witnessing my classmates struggle gives me a clear view of what happens when that education is absent. I am uniquely positioned to bridge these two experiences — and to explore how we can better prepare students for financial independence.

Bibliography

Annotated Sources

Akhtar, Muhammad, Syed Muhammad Ziyad Hameed, and Rehan Aftab

"Financial Fragility, Changing Face of Academia and Their Role in the Dissemination of Financial Literacy." Abasyn Journal of Social Sciences, 2021.

This peer-reviewed article examines how financial fragility plays into higher education and the role academia plays in promoting financial literacy. The authors argue that universities are uniquely positioned to address students' financial fragility, but that increasing commercialization and a reduced focus on practical life skills have undermined this potential. Particularly relevant for examining institutional responsibility in financial education.

Almeida, Fernando, and Orlando Costa

"Perspectives on Financial Literacy in Undergraduate Students." Journal of Education for Business, 2021.

Identifies gaps in undergraduate students' financial knowledge across budgeting, credit, investment, and long-term planning. The authors find that students recognize the importance of financial literacy but rely mainly on informal sources such as family and peers. Calls for formal financial literacy curriculum in undergraduate programs.

Barst, Anthony

Telephone interview. February 18, 2026.

A financial advisor at Morgan Stanley, Mr. Barst offered professional insights into real-world financial illiteracy. He noted that many college-aged children of his clients regularly ask him about basic topics — debt, budgeting, loans, investing — that he believes should have been taught in school. This interview bridges academic research and everyday consequences observed by finance professionals.

Davila, Hipolito, and Jitka Hilliard

"Financial Literacy and Student Debt: Survey of College Students." SSRN Electronic Journal, 2024.

Presents survey findings linking lower financial literacy to higher student debt and reduced awareness of long-term borrowing implications. Students with lower literacy were less likely to understand the true cost of minimum payments — directly relevant to the argument for mandatory financial education.

Faulkner, Ash

"Financial Literacy Around the World." The Reference Librarian, 2021.

Compares international approaches to financial literacy, finding that top-performing nations incorporate financial education early in schooling and support it through government policy and multiple social institutions. Provides global context for understanding why American financial literacy consistently lags behind peer nations.

Hummas, Shane

"Top 5 Personal Finance Tips for College Students." YouTube, 2021.

Presents practical, accessible personal finance advice for college students covering budgeting, credit, and saving habits. Represents the kind of informal digital content students turn to when formal education is absent — illustrating how YouTube has filled a gap left by traditional institutions.

Mohd Padil, Hazlina, et al.

"Financial Literacy and Awareness of Investment Scams Among University Students." Journal of Financial Crime, 2021.

Finds that university students with lower financial literacy are significantly more susceptible to investment scams and fraud — a risk compounded by increasing engagement with neobanks and cryptocurrency platforms. Adds a real-world crime dimension to the financial literacy problem.

Mudzingiri, Calvin

"The Impact of Financial Literacy on Risk Seeking and Patient Attitudes of University Students." Development Southern Africa, 2021.

Demonstrates that higher financial literacy is associated with more patient, long-term financial behavior. Suggests financial education shapes not just knowledge but fundamental behavioral and psychological patterns — its value extends well beyond what people formally know about money.

Vaaler, Alyson, Lauren Reiter, and Ash E. Faulkner

"They Seek, But Do They Find?" College & Research Libraries, 2021.

Finds that college students are motivated to seek out financial information but consistently struggle to locate content that is credible and actionable. Identifies a significant gap between students' effort and the quality of resources they find.

Vox

"Why Financial Literacy Education in the US Sucks." YouTube, 2025.

A documentary-style investigation tracing the roots of U.S. financial literacy underperformance to Cold War–era curriculum priorities that sidelined practical life skills. Connects financial illiteracy to measurable consequences — high personal debt, poor savings rates, and uninformed investment decisions.
